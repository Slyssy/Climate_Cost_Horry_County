FEMA and Household Finances in Horry County, SC

Introduction



Horry County lies in the northeastern coastal region of South Carolina. The natural landscape is characterized by coastal, marsh, and forested ecosystems, Horry. Human activity has brought farming and paved surfaces to the landscape. Due to it's location Horry County's location on the Atlantic Coast, and and the presence of multiple watersheds fed by the Waccamaw, Lumber, and Little Pee Dee, and Great Pee Dee Rivers, Horry County has always been prone to flooding.

However, in the past five years, residents of Horry County have begun to experience more severe flooding due to increasing frequency of hurricanes and heavy rainfall. Since 2016, Horry County has been directly hit by two major hurricanes, and impacted by severe winds and tropical storms from eight additional hurricanes.

The damaged caused by the increased rainfall can be seen in the money paid out by the federal government for storm recovery. The number and size of FEMA payouts over the years, which have far exceeded the growth in the population.

On behalf of the flood survivor advocacy group, Horry County Rising, the Climate Cost Project survey the residents of Horry County to understand how their livelihood has been affected by flooding. The analysis relied on a household economic survey (n=127) of flood survivors, and follow-up phone interviews (n=15) of a subsample of households to validate results and obtain more context on survey response.

Flood Risk Mapping

What is a flood risk zone, and how is it different from a flood zone?

Updated flood mapping needs to reflect the real risk of flooding. At present, the highest risk FEMA-designated flood zones are the V and A designated Special Hazard Flood Areas. These zones are supposed to indicate a one percent chance per year of flooding, with additional hazards implied in the V designated zones due to their coastal locations and susceptibility to wave action.

Houses and infrastructure that were designed to withstand flooding once every 100 years are now flooding more frequently. Housing and infrastructure development was generally avoided in flood zones where the chance of flooding was significantly greater than one percent per year. But the reality has changed so that much existing infrastructure and housing, as well as planned new developments, are now in high- risk zones for repeat flooding, for which there is no designation in FEMA’s Special Hazard Flood Maps.

The County’s ability to update its maps is limited by the lack of the descriptive power in FEMA’s current flood hazard rating system, which, with its implicit assumption of construction compliance for 100-year floods, does not capture real flood risk in extant and developing communities that are facing repeat flooding and climate change. This is a known problem with FEMA’s flood maps. Horry County is yet another example of a situation where the consequences of these problems are being felt in the deteriorating finances and security of its residents.

What can be done?

The City of Horry County is currently in the process of setting up supplemental flood zones that will better understand and define flood risk.

Avoiding future construction in high risk areas will both help to make room for future residents of the county, and help leave more land to absorb flood waters.

Using the most up-to-date information about how flood zones will change in the future in order to make planning decisions can also help to keep people the county out of high water. The National Atmospheric and Oceanic Administration also has a different set of flood risk maps than FEMA. Incorporating this information into the county's flood maps can help to improve assessment of risk.

Making Room for Water

Recent development in Horry County has been driven by population growth. The county's population has grown by 38.4% percent, from 270,274 thousand people to 373,033 people. It is expected to grow by an additional 270,000 by 2040. For this reason, simply avoiding construction may not be viable strategy for the county. However, careful planning of infrastructure in new and existing buildings that accommodates for the water in the landscape can help with flood protection.
