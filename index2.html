Introduction



Horry County lies in the northeastern coastal region of South Carolina. The natural landscape is characterized by coastal, marsh, and forested ecosystems, Horry. Human activity has brought farming and paved surfaces to the landscape. Due to it's location Horry County's location on

The Atlantic Coast, and and the presence of multiple watersheds fed by the Waccamaw, Lumber, and Little Pee Dee, and Great Pee Dee Rivers, Horry County has always been prone to flooding. However, in the past five years, residents of Horry County have begun to experience more severe flooding due to increasing frequency of hurricanes and heavy rainfall. Since 2016, Horry County has been directly hit by two major hurricanes, and impacted by severe winds and tropical storms from eight additional hurricanes.

The damaged caused by the increased rainfall can be seen in the money paid out by the federal government for storm recovery. The number and size of FEMA payouts over the years, which have far exceeded the growth in the population.

On behalf of the flood survivor advocacy group, Horry County Rising, the Climate Cost Project survey the residents of Horry County to understand how their livelihood has been affected by flooding. The analysis relied on a household economic survey (n=127) of flood survivors, and follow-up phone interviews (n=15) of a subsample of households to validate results and obtain more context on survey response.

Financial Impact

The survey revealed significant financial harm to households, and found that having flood insurance or receiving financial aid did not protect houses from significant financial harm.
